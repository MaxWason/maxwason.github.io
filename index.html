About

Results Oriented

Efficiency is working quickly towards a goal. Effectiveness is more concerned with working towards the rights goals, while still being expedient. This is the wellspring of innovation and meaningful accomplishments; it is the antithesis of busywork. Proactively designing better systems and not just reactively fixing symptoms, focusing on the results the whole time, is the only way to make something that is truly successful. Have the capability to think big picture, tempered with the skills and disciple to make it a reality.

Perspective

People Matter

Everything we make was made by and for people, they are the starting and end point for all we do. We must have empathy for the consumer as well as for our coworkers. We must know how people work (ideally data driven), and we must accomodate our findings. It is all too common in the technical field, and it feels a bit rediculous that it even needs to be said, but the impact of humans should not be forgotten.

Other

I honestly enjoy so many things, so I can't begin to cover them all. From a variety of sports to cooking to reading to programming, I am never at a lack of things to do. Fundamentally however, I have been pursuing the question of "What is a good life?" Which is a deceptively difficult question, as it seems hard to answer, but it is actually closer to impossible in reality. Life has too many confounding variables. And yet, it is worth it, to try and optimize my life is surprisingly similar in process to min-maxing a program's efficincy. Lots of tweaking, lots and lots of work, a little swearing, and a deep sense of satisfaction when making progress. Ultimately I hope to answer the question of what constitues a good life not only to benefit myself, but so that I can also share it with others and empower them in the process. To be a little more concrete, I'll go over two of the examples listed above: cooking and reading.


I was the head of the day shift, working a sous chef, in one of the top 100 restaurants in North America for a year, and the skills I learned there allow me to enjoy cooking in my own time. This also transfers nicely to my skills as a river guide; where I enjoy being completely disconnected from technology while helping disadvantaged youth have the time of their life, and hopefully learn something in the process.


I also really enjoy reading, mostly articles/blogs and some books as well. Typically it falls into three genres: professional research, escapist fiction, or nonfiction psychology. Some books I've really enjoyed recently (and can recommend) have been "So Good They Can't Ignore You" by Cal Newport, "Designing Games" by Tynan Sylvester, and "The Kingkiller Chronicles" by Patrick Rothfuss. Books that I intend to read soon include "Mindset" by Carol Dweck, "Dollars and Sense" by Dan Ariely and "Good Business" by Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi.
